### Confirmed (proposal items where I agree)

- **A (Diagnose 24 missed families)**: The correct first step. The example missed families cluster at chr4:10.5–11.1 Mb, which is the heterochromatic knob centered at ~2 Mb and the pericentromeric block centered at ~4 Mb on chr4 — exactly where ATHILA gypsy-type retrotransposons and TSI elements are densely packed (supported by PMC7385966 and the cytogenetic literature on chr4 structure). Elements of 358–713 bp with 15–17 copies sit right in the size range of: (a) MITE non-autonomous TIR elements (Wicker Class II, Order TIR; typical length 100–500 bp, copy numbers hundreds to thousands), (b) solo LTRs orphaned from their internal sequence by intrastrand recombination, and (c) TRIM/LARD non-autonomous LTR derivatives. Without knowing which of these three categories they fall into, no algorithmic fix can be properly targeted. Diagnosis is mandatory.

- **B (Nested containment fraction bug)**: Real bug, biologically important. LTR retrotransposons in plants routinely carry nested DNA transposon or MITE insertions within their internal regions (RepeatModeler2 notes this explicitly; Ou et al. 2019 addressed it via reciprocal filtering). A 2194 bp family being a structural prefix of a 2897 bp family is exactly the topology of a LINE or internal LTR domain plus a nested MITE. The merge gate correctly identifies the containment relationship but then incorrectly eliminates the shorter family instead of separating the two. The biological fix is also the engineering fix: the gate must allow both to survive, not promote the longer at the expense of the shorter.

- **C (Otsu split fires 0 times)**: Biologically plausible concern. The Gypsy superfamily in Arabidopsis is well documented to contain distinct lineages (ATHILA, TAT, Reina, Tat/Angela, CRM-related; see PMC7385966 reviewed elements). Within a single de novo consensus built by merging divergent gypsy-class copies, you should expect bimodal divergence distributions reflecting separate insertion waves. That the Otsu test fires zero times on chr4 is suspicious. However, the right explanation to rule out first is that the seed-and-extend pipeline may be producing clean, single-mode families to begin with (because it seeds on the highest-frequency k-mer per family and extends conservatively). If the tool is already inherently splitting subfamilies through separate seed-extension runs, Otsu being dormant is not pathological. Item C correctly asks for instrumentation before action. Agreed with priority.

- **G (Loosen merge length-ratio guard)**: Directionally consistent with TE biology. The Stage B fix already set 0.70 as a sensible starting point; loosening further to 0.50 should only be done after confirming what the current 0.70 threshold is blocking. A 500 bp element merging with a 1000 bp element (ratio 0.50) most likely represents a solo LTR being incorrectly merged with the full-length element's internal consensus — which should NOT be merged. So loosening this specific guard may actually HURT biological accuracy.

- **Tier 4 exclusions**: Correct. RECON-style self-alignment and TRF integration are out of scope by design philosophy. This boundary is biologically well-motivated: mdl-repeat's design target is high-copy active TEs, not the ancient fragment landscape that RECON handles.

### Concerns / disagreements

- **D (Multi-pass on residual genome)**: The biological rationale is partially valid but contains a hidden assumption that may not hold. The argument is that dominant high-copy elements exhaust MAXR slots and starve second-tier families. STAGE_A_REPORT Hypothesis B tested this and found it WEAK: the victim family achieved identical recall with or without the 100-copy dominant present. Additionally, the "residual genome" concept has a TE biology complication: if an LTR retrotransposon family accounts for 40% of pericentric sequence, masking it in Pass 1 creates a discontinuous residual genome full of gaps. Pass 2 seeds on this gapped sequence and the l-mer frequency distribution becomes unreliable because many l-mers now span masked-to-unmasked boundaries. The multi-pass approach is standard in RepeatModeler2's philosophy, but RepeatModeler2 uses full genome for each pass; it does not seed on the residual. Masking and re-running risks conflating "novel families discovered on residual" with "fragments of already-found families that span masked boundaries." If multi-pass is implemented, it should seed on the FULL genome each time and use the growing library as an exclusion filter at the acceptance stage, not at the seeding stage.

- **E (Repeat-discriminative seed scoring)**: The proposal frames this as "Pass 1 finds repeat-like regions; Pass 2 uses those as a profile." This is structurally similar to what EDTA does (and to a lesser extent RepeatModeler2). The biological concern is circularity: if the "repeat-like profile" is anchored to Pass 1 families, it will strongly favor re-discovering the same families in Pass 2 and will not help with the novel / divergent families that Pass 1 missed. For those novel families (which are the ones in the recall gap), their k-mer profiles are precisely NOT represented in the Pass 1 library. The minimizer/syncmer variant (more uniform sampling) is the biologically sounder idea here and does not suffer from this circularity problem.

- **F (BLAST-based instance recruitment)**: The biological concern is the opposite direction from what the proposal states. The proposal says "BLAST handles diverged instances better." This is true, but the design scope is explicitly "low divergence" (active TEs). At 80% identity, dc-megablast and the current banded DP should perform comparably; the precision gain at 90×80 attributed to BLAST is unlikely to be large on Arabidopsis data where most in-scope elements are recently active and not highly diverged. Where BLAST would genuinely help is for elements in the 200–500 bp length range (see Stage B length-stratified recall: these are the bins with the largest remaining gap). Short elements with indels are poorly handled by banded DP with a fixed band width, and BLAST's HSP extension with affine gaps is meaningfully better here. The biological framing should reference element length, not divergence level.

- **H (Loosen Stage B fallback: 3 copies → 2 copies)**: Biologically problematic. The design scope states "≥3 copies" explicitly. Reducing to 2 copies admits elements that are within the scope of RECON-style tools by the project's own definition. More importantly, in repeat-dense pericentromeric regions, a 2-copy "family" is statistically indistinguishable from a spurious alignment artifact between two nearly-identical elements that happen to share a recent insertion. The Arabidopsis chr4 pericentric block has ~20–30% TE content (confirmed by PMC7385966), which means many 2-copy coincidences. The FP rate increase would be concentrated in exactly the region where the evaluation truth is most uncertain (CEN180 and ATHILA are co-localised). The 3-copy floor is a biologically motivated floor, not an arbitrary parameter. Do not loosen unless a specific biological reason (e.g., a small genome test case) is given.

- **I (Loosen align max-divergence: 0.30 → 0.40)**: Already tested empirically (HANDOFF §4: "F1 unchanged"). The proposal lists this as a Tier 3 parameter tune. The HANDOFF dead-ends explicitly say "don't retry without new evidence." Repeating this experiment would waste time. However, there is one untested variant: applying the increased divergence threshold ONLY to elements that pass the MDL standalone gate (Fix 1 from Stage B), not globally. Globally, divergence 0.40 adds noise; but for families that have already passed standalone MDL, it may help capture additional diverged copies without increasing FP on noise. This nuance is worth noting.

- **C (Otsu thresholds: REFINE_BIMODALITY_THRESH 0.20 → 0.10 / REFINE_MIN_DIV_GAP 0.05 → 0.03)**: The proposal's proposed relaxation is arbitrary. Before changing thresholds, the instrumentation step is correct — but the expected output of the instrumentation should be checked against what is biologically expected. In Arabidopsis, gypsy retrotransposons like ATHILA are in high copy and show distinct insertion waves separated by millions of years, so bimodal divergence IS expected. Copia elements (ATCOPIA78, ATCOPIA93) show recent (<1 My) insertion bursts and should produce narrow, near-zero divergence peaks, not bimodal distributions, because they have not had time to diverge. If Otsu fires zero times, it may be because the currently discovered families are predominantly young Copia-class families (low divergence, no second mode) rather than that the threshold is wrong. This interpretation deserves checking against the family composition before blindly relaxing thresholds.

### NEW action items not in proposal

**BIO-N1: Characterize the 24 missed families by TE class before any algorithm change.**
The chr4:10.5–11.1 Mb cluster of 15–17-copy, 358–713 bp missed elements sits in the vicinity of a known heterochromatic knob (~2 Mb) and the pericentric block (~4–6.5 Mb) on chr4. Elements of 358–470 bp at this location are the size range of: (a) solo LTRs (LTR terminal repeats from gypsy-class elements are typically 300–700 bp; Athila LTRs are ~750 bp but TATelements' LTRs are shorter), (b) MITE non-autonomous TIR elements (Wicker Class II, Order TIR; Arabidopsis has >1000-copy MITE families: ATREP3 1439 copies, HELITRONY3 1399 copies, ATREP10D 1295 copies, ATREP15 1003 copies, per PMC7385966), and (c) small non-autonomous LTR derivatives (TRIM elements, ~200–600 bp, carry intact LTRs but minimal internal sequence). Extract the sequences with bedtools, BLAST them against Dfam or Repbase, and classify. If they are MITEs or solo LTRs, the algorithmic response is very different:
  - Solo LTRs: a structural-aware LTR detection module (LTR_retriever-style) should detect the terminal repeats; seed-and-extend is not the right tool.
  - MITEs: short, high-copy, TIR-flanked; their seeds are k-mers that span the TIR, which are short and may fall below l-mer length (k=14 for chr4). The natural fix is a shorter k for these elements specifically, or a TIR-seeding mode.
  - TRIM elements: same structural issue as solo LTRs.

**BIO-N2: Solo LTR handling — the LTR internal region and its solo LTR are biologically distinct but share sequence at the LTR boundary; treat them as distinct families.**
RepeatModeler2 (PMC7196820) explicitly states: "LTRs are often identified as fragments with disassociated LTR and internal regions or missing the internal segment completely using the RepeatScout/RECON methodologies." The current merge gate attempts to prevent incorrect merging of a solo LTR with the full-length element, but Stage A shows the Genome C test (which is exactly this topology) fails with 0/4 recall because of a merge gate inversion bug. Once the Genome C bug is fixed (Proposal B), the pipeline must then be tested on a known full-length LTR retrotransposon family where solo LTRs are also present. The expected correct behavior is: two output families — one for the internal region + flanking LTRs (consensus ~5–18 kb), one for the solo LTR alone (consensus ~300–750 bp). If the pipeline collapses them into one under-representative family (as it currently does), both are missed at their respective truth intervals. This is likely a major contributor to the chr4 recall gap for the >5 kb bin (recall 0.000 at >10 kb in Stage A/B) and a contributor to the 500–1000 bp bin as well.

**BIO-N3: MITE-specific seeding strategy — k-mer length is likely too long for short TIR elements.**
MITEs in Arabidopsis are 100–500 bp with copy numbers of 500–10,000 (per MITE literature and PMC7385966 data showing four Helitron/MITE families at >1000 copies each). The default l-mer length for chr4 (18.6 Mb) is ceil(1 + log4(18.6e6)) ≈ 13–14 bp. For a 358 bp element, a 14 bp seed is not problematic per se, but the k-mer must be drawn from the element-internal sequence, not from the TIR. For MITEs, the TIRs are the most conserved region (high frequency across all copies) and the internal sequence is often not well-conserved across family members. If the l-mer hash is capturing TIR k-mers, extension will produce a consensus dominated by TIR sequence and fail to converge. Validate: for a known MITE family (e.g., Arabidopsis Stowaway-like element ~250 bp), check whether the seed l-mers chosen are in the TIR or the internal region. If in the TIR, the extension will fail because TIR k-mers appear in both orientations and at both ends. This is a structural seeding problem unique to palindromic TIR elements that RepeatScout-style tools are known to mishandle.

**BIO-N4: Evaluate the truth set quality for the 358–470 bp missed families specifically.**
The truth BED is derived from Ensembl Plants TAIR10 soft-masked positions. CEN180 (~178 bp monomers) is included in the truth and explicitly noted as out-of-scope (HANDOFF §0). The 358 bp and 470 bp elements are suspiciously close to 2× and 2.5× CEN180 monomer lengths. Before attributing missed recall to algorithm failure, verify that these specific intervals are not: (a) low-complexity tandem arrays that passed the periodicity filter by chance (they are 358–713 bp, which could be 2–4 CEN180 monomers), (b) TSI sequences (the 3′ half of an Athila retrotransposon that forms a distinct tandem array at Arabidopsis centromeres and pericentromeres — this is explicitly documented in the chr4 heterochromatin literature and would be out-of-scope). If any of the 24 missed families are actually TSI or CEN180-variant, they should be excluded from the recall numerator, which would raise the true in-scope recall.

**BIO-N5: Strand handling for asymmetric TE families.**
The current benchmark uses `--ignore-strand` in `bed_pr.py`, which is correct for MITE/TIR elements (palindromic, detected on either strand) and for LINEs (which insert non-directionally relative to gene orientation). However, for LTR retrotransposons, the canonical strand of the element is biologically meaningful: the 5' LTR is the promoter, the 3' LTR is the polyadenylation signal. RepeatMasker annotates strand in its output. If the library consensus is built from the minus-strand orientation for some families, `--ignore-strand` masks a quality defect: the consensus is reverse-complement of the canonical form, which will cause misidentification during annotation. Verify that the library consensuses are all in the canonical (+) orientation defined by the RT/RH domain reading frame. This is not a recall problem but a library quality problem that would cause errors in downstream RepeatMasker-based annotation and TE age estimation (CpG methylation decay, K-divergence dating requires correct orientation).

**BIO-N6: CRM-class centromeric retrotransposons — reconsider scope boundary.**
The HANDOFF correctly excludes CEN180 (tandem satellite) as out-of-scope. However, CRM (Centromere Retrotransposon of Maize) / ATHILA elements are LTR retrotransposons that preferentially insert into centromeres (their integrase has a chromodomain targeting H3K9me2; confirmed in plant biology literature). In Arabidopsis, ATHILA2 has 413 copies and ATHILA4A has 310 copies (per PMC7385966), and they are found almost exclusively in pericentromeric regions. These are NOT tandem repeats; they are interspersed LTR retrotransposons and are squarely within the Wicker Class I / Order LTR / Superfamily Gypsy scope. The current scope exclusion of "centromeric regions" in the benchmark (the HANDOFF notes "Inside cen: recall 0.699 — high-copy LTR mostly OK; CEN180 missed by design") conflates the satellite (CEN180, out of scope) with the interspersed ATHILA elements (in scope). If ATHILA families are being evaluated in the "Inside cen" bucket and showing good recall (0.699), that is already in-scope and correct. But if any ATHILA instances in the pericentromere are being excluded from the denominator along with CEN180, the recall denominator is understated. Clarify whether the chr4_full_truth.bed stratification by "inside/outside centromere" excludes ATHILA instances.

### Suggested re-prioritization

The proposal's ordering (A → B → D → C → F → E → G/H/I/J) is reasonable but should be adjusted based on biology:

**Revised order**:

1. **A + BIO-N1 together** (1 day): Run diagnostic on 24 missed families AND classify them by TE type. These two are the same experiment. The classification result (MITE vs solo LTR vs TSI variant) directly determines which of B, BIO-N2, BIO-N3, or BIO-N4 to execute next. Without this, B and C may be fixing the wrong problem.

2. **B (Genome C merge gate bug)** (0.5 day): Fix the nested-containment inversion bug. The Genome C failure is a known bug with a bounded fix. It blocks any test of the solo LTR / nested TE biology.

3. **BIO-N2 (Test solo LTR handling)** (0.5 day, test only, after B is fixed): After fixing the merge gate bug, create a synthetic genome with a known full-length LTR retrotransposon plus solo LTRs (can use actual ATHILA sequence from chr4) and verify the pipeline produces two distinct families. This is a go/no-go gate for any recall improvement in the 500–1000 bp range.

4. **C (Instrument Otsu split)** (0.5 day instrumentation, 1 day fix): After BIO-N1 diagnosis, if the missed families include gypsy-class elements with diverged subfamilies, instrument and fix. If the missed families are MITEs / solo LTRs, skip C and go to BIO-N3.

5. **BIO-N3 (MITE seeding)**: If BIO-N1 identifies MITEs as a major missing class, this is the appropriate fix. Effort depends on whether a TIR-seeding mode is added or whether only the l-mer selection is modified.

6. **D (Multi-pass)**: Lower priority than the above. The biological rationale is weaker than the proposal suggests (STAGE_A shows cross-family interference is not the bottleneck). If implemented, use full-genome seeding each pass, not residual-genome seeding.

7. **F, E**: As proposed; F before E.

8. **G**: Do NOT loosen the length-ratio guard below 0.70 without checking whether what is being blocked is specifically solo-LTR-to-full-element incorrect merges. This guard may be protecting a biologically correct separation.

9. **H**: Do NOT implement (3 → 2 copy floor). Conflicts with design scope.

10. **I**: Already empirically tested and failed (HANDOFF). Do not retry globally. Consider per-family conditional relaxation only.

### Open questions for the proposer

1. **What is the TE class of the 24 missed families?** This is the most important unknown. BLAST the representative sequences (from the QUALITY_PROPOSAL's chr4:10605713–10606425 and similar coordinates) against Dfam or Repbase. The answer determines whether the right fix is algorithmic (seed selection), structural (LTR_retriever-style module), or evaluation-related (elements are out-of-scope TSI/CEN-related).

2. **Does the truth BED on chr4 contain solo LTR intervals as distinct truth families, or are they merged with the full-length LTR retrotransposon intervals?** If the truth BED was derived from RepeatMasker soft-masking using Repbase/Dfam, solo LTRs and full-length elements share the same family name (e.g., ATHILA) and are in the same truth interval pool. In that case, even if mdl-repeat never produces a solo LTR consensus, it may still mask solo LTR positions if the full-length element consensus is good enough — because RepeatMasker will match the LTR region of the full-length consensus against the solo LTR. This needs to be checked: are the 24 missed families' truth intervals in the same RM family as intervals that ARE covered, or in distinct families?

3. **Is the library outputting full-length LTR retrotransposon consensuses at all for ATHILA?** The STAGE_A/B length-bin data shows recall = 0.000 for the >10 kb bin consistently. ATHILA internal regions are ~11 kb. If no family consensus is reaching >10 kb, the pipeline may be truncating LTR internal regions before completion. The ALIGN_MAX_EXTENSION = 10000 parameter (Proposal J) is a plausible culprit: if the element is 11 kb and the extension limit is 10 kb, the consensus is truncated. Check the maximum consensus length in the output library for chr4.

4. **What happens to the Genome C failure after the merge gate bug is fixed?** STAGE_A identifies the root cause as incorrect merge gate behavior for a LINE (2194 bp) vs LINE+LTR composite (2897 bp). This is exactly the structure you would expect in a pericentromeric region where LTR retrotransposons are nested inside each other (ATHILA elements are known to carry nested insertions of smaller elements). Once fixed, does testD (the production test) still pass, and does Genome C give the expected 2 families?

5. **Is tandem coalesce (20x factor) contributing to false positives in the CEN180-adjacent regions?** The tandem coalesce transform merges adjacent same-family same-strand instances at up to 20x the consensus length. In pericentric regions where ATHILA and TSI elements are adjacent and share sequence similarity, this could produce false mega-intervals that inflate the "Inside cen" precision/recall numbers in a misleading direction. This is specifically relevant if the coalesce factor is producing intervals that span CEN180 satellites (which would be true positives in the truth BED but are biologically a different repeat class). The benchmark should stratify "intervals containing coalesced transitions" separately.
